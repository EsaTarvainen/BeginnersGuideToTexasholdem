Beginner tips

Start with strong hands

A simple way to improve as a new player is to play only strong starting hands. Good hands such as high pairs and high-card combinations like A-K or A-Q win more often and are easier to play post-flop. Connected cards of the same suit, suited connectors, and small pairs also have a lot of potential, but if overplayed, can get you into trouble. Poker rewards patience. Folding weak hands pre-flop will keep you away from difficult spots and increase your success significantly in the long run.

As you gain experience, you'll learn to think in terms of ranges rather than exact hands. A range is a selection of hands a player could realistically have. Your own opening range should be tight when you are in early position, because there are many players still left to act who might hold stronger hands. The more players there are at the table, and the earlier your position, the stronger your hand should be before entering a pot. A late position is a big advantage in poker because you get to act after others and therefore have more information before making decisions.

Thinking about your opponents' ranges is just as important as choosing your own. Every action such as raising, calling, or checking, narrows down which types of hands they might hold. Comparing your likely range to your opponents' is how poker is played at higher levels. Adopting this “range vs range” mindset will help you make better decisions throughout the hand.

Betting

A bet in poker should always have a clear purpose, and there are three main reasons to bet:
A value bet is when you bet with a strong hand because you expect weaker hands to call you. You win more chips by getting paid. A value bet can also be used to protect your hand, denying a chance for your opponents to improve for free by getting a new community card.
A bluff is when you bet with a weak hand to make stronger hands fold. Here a tight perceived range will help you to get opponents to fold.
A semi bluff is a bet with a hand that is behind right now but has good ways to improve on later cards, giving you two ways to win: either your opponent folds now or you hit a stronger hand later.

When choosing your bet size, always compare it to the size of the pot. Bigger bets create more pressure and make it harder for opponents to continue, while smaller bets risk fewer chips and can still win value with decent hands. Thinking about bet size as a percentage of the pot helps keep your decisions consistent.

Don't call too often

A very common beginner mistake is calling way too often in the hope that “the right card” arrives on the turn or river. Only call when you already have something, like a made pair, or when you have many ways to improve. Those improvement cards are called outs, specific cards left in the deck that would make your hand stronger. A draw is a hand that isn't strong yet but can become strong if the right card arrives (e.g., four to a straight or four to a flush). For a call to be worth it, your outs should realistically make a strong hand when they hit. Before you put chips in, always think about your opponent's range (the set of hands they could have) and ask yourself: “What kinds of hands would they bet this size with here?” If that likely range is too strong, folding is usually the better choice.
